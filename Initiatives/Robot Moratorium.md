## ORDINANCE NO. 2026-__

**AN INITIATED ORDINANCE OF THE PEOPLE OF THE TOWN OF PAONIA, COLORADO, ESTABLISHING A MORATORIUM ON AUTONOMOUS ROBOTS IN PUBLIC SPACES, PROTECTING PUBLIC SAFETY AND PRIVACY, AND PROVIDING FOR ENFORCEMENT AND REVIEW**

### RECITALS

**WHEREAS**, the Town of Paonia is a statutory municipality organized under C.R.S. § 31-1-203; and

**WHEREAS**, C.R.S. §§ 31-15-101 and 31-15-401 grant statutory municipalities police-power authority to protect the health, safety, and welfare of its citizens; and

**WHEREAS**, in June 2025, autonomous camera-equipped robots manufactured by Daxbot, Inc. were deployed on Paonia streets and sidewalks without advance public notice through established channels, without a dedicated public hearing on the technology, and without opportunity for citizen input on the deployment; and

**WHEREAS**, the robots were equipped with cameras, GPS, laser sensors, and cellular connectivity, and the data collection capabilities, data storage and retention practices, and third-party data sharing arrangements were not disclosed to the public before, during, or after deployment; and

**WHEREAS**, the robot manufacturer, Daxbot, Inc., produces both ADA assessment robots and autonomous security patrol robots on a substantially similar hardware platform, and this dual-use nature of the technology was not disclosed to the public; and

**WHEREAS**, no formal safety assessment was conducted or published prior to operating autonomous machines on public sidewalks shared with pedestrians, children, elderly residents, and persons with disabilities; and

**WHEREAS**, Colorado has no regulatory framework governing autonomous robots on public sidewalks, as the only proposed legislation (SB 20-092, 2020) was postponed indefinitely and never became law; and

**WHEREAS**, the People of Paonia have the right to require a public process before data-collecting autonomous technology is deployed in their community;

**NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT ORDAINED BY THE PEOPLE OF THE TOWN OF PAONIA, COLORADO:**

### Section 1. Title.

This initiated ordinance shall be known and cited as the "Autonomous Robot Moratorium."

### Section 2. Definitions.

For purposes of this ordinance:

(a) **"Autonomous robot"** means any self-propelled mechanical device that operates in a public space without continuous direct human control, including ground-based, aerial, and aquatic devices. This includes devices capable of navigating, collecting data, recording, or transmitting information while operating autonomously.

(b) **"Public space"** means any area accessible to the general public, including streets, sidewalks, parks, trails, alleys, and public rights-of-way.

(c) **"Continuous direct human control"** means real-time operation by a human operator who directs all movement and functions of the device throughout its operation, as distinguished from pre-programmed or algorithmic navigation.

### Section 3. Moratorium.

(a) **Prohibition.** During the moratorium period, no person or entity shall operate an autonomous robot in any public space within the Town limits.

(b) **No fees or permits.** This ordinance does not establish, require, or authorize any fees, licenses, or permits.

(c) **Exception.** Robots deployed by a public safety agency in active response to a specific emergency, under continuous direct human control by a trained operator, are exempt from this prohibition.

### Section 4. Federal Compliance.

Nothing in this ordinance shall be construed to prohibit or impede the Town's compliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act or any other federal or state law. The Town may fulfill any such obligation using methods that do not require autonomous robots.

### Section 5. Moratorium Period.

(a) The moratorium established by this ordinance expires on December 31, 2028.

(b) Prior to expiration, the Town is encouraged to develop a regulatory framework for autonomous robots through a public process that includes public notice, public hearings, and opportunity for citizen comment.

(c) If no regulatory framework is adopted before December 31, 2028, the moratorium expires and no regulation of autonomous robots shall be in effect unless separately enacted.

### Section 6. Enforcement.

(a) Violations of this ordinance constitute a civil infraction.

(b) Any registered elector of the Town may report a violation to the Town.

(c) Any registered elector of the Town may seek injunctive relief in Delta County District Court to enforce this ordinance. A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to reasonable attorney fees and costs.

### Section 7. Severability.

If any provision of this ordinance is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect.

### Section 8. Effective Date.

This ordinance takes effect thirty (30) days after certification of election results.

**Re: Submission of Citizen Initiative Petition — Autonomous Robot Moratorium**

Dear Town Clerk,

I hereby submit this citizen initiative petition proposing an ordinance that establishes a temporary moratorium on autonomous robots operating in public spaces within the Town of Paonia.

### I. THIS INITIATIVE IS LEGISLATIVE

The proposed ordinance is a textbook exercise of legislative power. It satisfies every element of the Blackwell test:

1. **Creates permanent law** — establishes a moratorium with defined duration, not a one-time directive
2. **Declares policy** — autonomous robots shall not operate in public spaces during the moratorium
3. **Applies generally** — binds all persons and entities equally, targets no individual operator

Temporary moratoria are among the most well-established forms of legislative action in Colorado municipal law. Towns routinely enact moratoria on development, land use, and commercial activity while regulatory frameworks are developed. *See, e.g.*, C.R.S. § 29-20-104 (land use moratorium authority). A moratorium on autonomous robots in public spaces is an exercise of the identical legislative power.

Under *Vagneur v. City of Aspen*, 295 P.3d 493 (Colo. 2013), the initiative power must be "liberally construed." This initiative could not be more clearly legislative: a temporary prohibition, a defined scope, an automatic sunset, an emergency exemption, and no administrative apparatus whatsoever.

**The initiative does NOT:**
- Direct specific administrative actions
- Target any individual or entity
- Create fees, licenses, or permits
- Interfere with day-to-day municipal operations

### II. PAONIA IS A STATUTORY TOWN

Paonia is organized under C.R.S. § 31-1-203 as a statutory municipality. Any legal analysis applying home rule limitations to this petition is inapplicable. The initiative power in statutory towns derives from state statute and must be construed in favor of the electorate.

### III. THE FACTUAL BASIS FOR THIS INITIATIVE

On June 17–18, 2025, multiple autonomous robots manufactured by Daxbot, Inc. were deployed on Paonia's public streets and sidewalks as part of an ADA compliance assessment contracted to Mile High Accessibility Consultants (MHAC) for $33,500. The following governance failures attended this deployment:

**Inadequate notice.** The Town posted on Facebook the morning of (or immediately before) deployment. No advance notice was published through established channels — no newspaper notice, no posted flyers, no multi-day advance warning. The robots arrived earlier than the Town expected; Town Administrator Stefen Wynn acknowledged the Town was "caught off guard." The Delta County Sheriff's Office was not notified — a responding deputy had no knowledge of the robots when called to the scene by a resident.

**No dedicated public hearing.** The Board tentatively accepted the MHAC contract at the March 24, 2025 regular meeting, but the discussion concerned cost comparison of seven bids, not the specific technology, its data collection methods, or its privacy implications. No public hearing was held on the decision to deploy autonomous camera-equipped robots on residential streets.

**No privacy disclosure.** The robots were equipped with cameras, centimeter-accurate GPS, laser sensors, photogrammetry capability, and cellular connectivity (Daxbot product specifications). The Town's Facebook post warned residents they would be "on camera" but provided no disclosure of what data was collected, where it is stored, how long it is retained, who has access, or whether it is shared with Daxbot, MHAC, or any third party. No privacy impact assessment was conducted or published. As of this submission, the Town has still not provided a complete public accounting of data handling practices.

**Undisclosed dual-use technology.** Daxbot, Inc. manufactures two product lines on a substantially similar "Helium-class" hardware platform: ADA assessment robots and autonomous security patrol robots featuring live surveillance, 24/7 remote monitoring, incident detection, and evidence capture for law enforcement. The Town never disclosed that the robots deployed in Paonia were built by a surveillance technology company on a dual-use platform.

**No safety assessment.** No formal safety assessment was conducted or published prior to deploying autonomous machines on streets and sidewalks shared with pedestrians, children, elderly residents, and persons with disabilities. The Town's Facebook post warned residents they "could be liable for damages" if they interfered with robots — shifting risk to the public rather than assessing it.

**No data use policy.** The Town has published no policy governing permissible uses of data collected by the robots, no access controls limiting who may review the imagery, and no restrictions preventing the data from being used for purposes unrelated to ADA compliance — including code enforcement, law enforcement, or sharing with third parties. In the absence of such a policy, nothing prevents robot-collected imagery of private property, residents, and businesses from being treated as general-purpose municipal intelligence.

**No state regulatory framework.** Colorado has no statute governing autonomous robots on public sidewalks. The only proposed legislation, SB 20-092 (2020), was postponed indefinitely and never became law. The Town deployed this technology into a regulatory vacuum.

This initiative exists because the Town deployed autonomous, camera-equipped, data-collecting robots on residential streets without adequate public notice, without a public hearing on the technology, without privacy disclosure, and without safety assessment. The People are exercising their constitutional right to impose a temporary pause and require that a proper public process occur before any future deployment.

### III-A. ADA COMPLIANCE IS NOT IMPEDED

The Town's ADA compliance obligation does not require the use of autonomous robots. The Town received seven proposals for ADA assessment services. Six of the seven bidders proposed conventional (human) assessment methods at prices ranging from $33,500 to $126,781. A moratorium on autonomous robots does not prevent the Town from completing or maintaining its ADA Transition Plan using non-robotic methods. This initiative regulates the *means* of data collection in public spaces, not the *obligation* to comply with federal accessibility law.
