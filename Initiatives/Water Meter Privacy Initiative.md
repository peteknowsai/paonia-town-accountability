## Summary
Prohibits the Town from installing water meters that collect high-frequency usage data or transmit data over cellular networks. Preserves existing radio-read and manual meters for billing. Protects residential utility privacy. Voter-only amendment/repeal.

## Budget Source
- **FY-2026 Adopted Budget, pages 75-76:** $500,000 over 5 years (FY-2026 through FY-2030)
- **FY-2026 initial investment:** $50,000
- **Approximate customers:** ~1,598
- **Funding:** 100% Water Fund (no grants)
- **Hidden recurring costs:** $50-136K/year in perpetual cellular subscriptions, platform licenses, maintenance

## Why This Matters
- 15-minute usage data reveals when you're home, away, sleeping, on vacation
- Cellular transmission = cybersecurity exposure for every household
- *Naperville Smart Meter Awareness v. City of Naperville* (7th Cir. 2018) — smart meter data collection IS a Fourth Amendment search
- Colorado HB25-1175 (2025) — state recognized privacy concerns for smart electric meters (opt-in required), but law doesn't cover municipal water
- Existing radio-read meters (purchased 2020-2021) work fine for billing
- Town simultaneously buying a meter-reading truck — contradicts claimed need for cellular
- $500K+ better spent on unfunded Phases 2-5 of 20-year water CIP

## Key Design Decisions
- **Doesn't ban water meters** — Section 3(c) explicitly preserves manual and drive-by radio-read meters
- **Defines "drive-by radio-read meter"** (Section 2(d)) — bright line between short-range radio-read (fine) and cellular-to-cloud (banned)
- **Data protection provisions** — billing/leak detection only, no third-party sharing, 12-month retention limit
- **Contracts void** — kills any existing purchase orders
- **No compliance period needed** — meters haven't been installed yet, this heads them off

# FULL TEXT: ORDINANCE

## ORDINANCE NO. 2026-__

**AN INITIATED ORDINANCE OF THE PEOPLE OF THE TOWN OF PAONIA, COLORADO, PROHIBITING SURVEILLANCE-CAPABLE WATER METERS AND PROTECTING RESIDENTIAL UTILITY PRIVACY**

### RECITALS

**WHEREAS**, the Town of Paonia is a statutory municipality organized under C.R.S. § 31-1-203; and

**WHEREAS**, C.R.S. §§ 31-15-101 and 31-15-401 grant statutory municipalities police-power authority to protect the health, safety, and welfare of its citizens; and

**WHEREAS**, residents of Paonia have a fundamental right to privacy in their homes, including the right to be free from government collection of data that reveals intimate details of daily life such as when residents are home, when they are away, when they sleep, and when they are on vacation; and

**WHEREAS**, the FY-2026 Adopted Budget allocates $500,000 over five years (FY-2026 through FY-2030) to replace the Town's existing radio-read water meters with cellular-connected meters capable of transmitting usage data at frequent intervals through third-party cellular networks to cloud-based platforms; and

**WHEREAS**, in addition to the $500,000 capital cost, cellular meters require perpetual monthly cellular data subscriptions, software platform licenses, and ongoing maintenance, creating estimated recurring costs of $50,000 to $136,000 per year that the Town will bear indefinitely; and

**WHEREAS**, the Town's existing radio-read meters are functional for billing purposes, having been purchased as recently as 2020-2021, and the Town is simultaneously purchasing a dedicated meter-reading vehicle, demonstrating that manual and drive-by meter reading remains operationally viable; and

**WHEREAS**, high-frequency water usage data constitutes a detailed record of household activity patterns that reveals far more about residents' private lives than is necessary for water billing, which requires only a single monthly reading; and

**WHEREAS**, the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit in Naperville Smart Meter Awareness v. City of Naperville (2018) held that the collection of smart meter data at frequent intervals constitutes a search under the Fourth Amendment because it reveals intimate details of what occurs within the home; and

**WHEREAS**, cellular transmission of residential utility data through third-party networks and cloud platforms creates cybersecurity vulnerabilities including interception, unauthorized access, and data breaches that could expose the private activity patterns of every household in the Town; and

**WHEREAS**, the Town has not publicly disclosed the identity of the cellular carrier, the cloud platform provider, the data retention policy, the data security standards, or whether any third party will have access to individualized residential usage data; and

**WHEREAS**, Colorado enacted HB25-1175 in 2025 requiring customer opt-in consent before installation of smart electric meters, reflecting a statewide policy recognition that smart meter technology implicates privacy interests, although this law does not apply to municipal water utilities; and

**WHEREAS**, the Town's water system faces critical infrastructure needs including unfunded Phases 2 through 5 of a twenty-year capital improvement plan, and the People believe that $500,000 in local Water Fund dollars plus perpetual recurring costs are better directed toward repairing pipes, tanks, and distribution infrastructure than toward surveillance-capable metering; and

**WHEREAS**, the purpose of water metering is to measure consumption for billing, and this purpose is fully served by the existing radio-read system and by drive-by meter reading without cellular connectivity or high-frequency data collection;

**NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT ORDAINED BY THE PEOPLE OF THE TOWN OF PAONIA, COLORADO:**

### Section 1. Title.

This initiated ordinance shall be known and cited as the "Residential Utility Privacy Act."

### Section 2. Definitions.

For purposes of this ordinance:

(a) **"Surveillance-capable meter"** means any water meter or metering device that: (i) collects, records, or stores water usage data at intervals more frequent than once per billing cycle; or (ii) transmits water usage data over cellular, radio-frequency, or other wireless networks to any server, platform, or system located off the premises of the metered property.

(b) **"Water usage data"** means any record of the volume, flow rate, or timing of water consumption at a service address.

(c) **"Billing cycle"** means the regular interval at which the Town issues water bills to customers, but not more frequently than once per calendar month.

(d) **"Drive-by radio-read meter"** means a water meter that stores cumulative usage data locally and transmits that data only in response to a short-range radio signal from a handheld or vehicle-mounted reader operated by Town personnel in physical proximity to the meter.

### Section 3. Prohibition.

(a) **No surveillance-capable meters.** The Town shall not purchase, install, or operate any surveillance-capable meter on any water service connection within the Town.

(b) **No third-party circumvention.** The Town shall not contract with or authorize any third party to install or operate surveillance-capable meters on the Town's behalf.

(c) **Existing metering preserved.** Nothing in this ordinance prohibits the Town from metering water usage for billing purposes using manually-read meters, drive-by radio-read meters, or any other metering method that does not meet the definition of surveillance-capable meter.

### Section 4. Data Protection.

(a) Water usage data collected by the Town shall be used solely for billing, leak detection, and water system management.

(b) The Town shall not sell, share, or disclose individualized water usage data to any third party except as required by a court order, subpoena, or express provision of state or federal law.

(c) The Town shall not retain individualized water usage data beyond the current billing cycle and the immediately preceding twelve (12) billing cycles.

### Section 5. Contracts Void.

Any contract, agreement, or expenditure of public funds for the purchase or installation of surveillance-capable meters that violates this ordinance is void and unenforceable.

### Section 6. Enforcement.

(a) Any registered elector of the Town may seek injunctive relief in Delta County District Court to enforce this ordinance.

(b) A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to reasonable attorney fees and costs.

### Section 7. Amendment and Repeal.

This ordinance may only be amended or repealed by vote of the registered electors of the Town.

### Section 8. Severability.

If any provision of this ordinance is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect.

### Section 9. Effective Date.

This ordinance takes effect thirty (30) days after certification of election results.
