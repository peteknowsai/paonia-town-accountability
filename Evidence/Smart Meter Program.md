# Smart Meter Program: Cellular Read Meter Replacement

## Overview

The Town is replacing its existing radio-read water meters with cellular-connected "smart" meters. This creates a permanent, recurring subscription cost that will grow every year as more meters are deployed.

## Budget Details (from FY-2026 Adopted Budget, pages 75-76)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| **Total program cost** | $500,000 |
| **FY-2026 initial investment** | $50,000 |
| **Program duration** | FY-2026 through FY-2030 (5 years) |
| **Funding source** | Water Fund (100% local — no grants) |
| **Approximate customers** | ~1,598 |
| **Cost per meter (hardware + install)** | ~$313 each (if $500K / 1,598) |

## The Recurring Cost Problem

### What the budget says:
> "The initial investment of $50,000 in FY-2026 begins a multiyear, systematic change out of aging meters, with recurring annual investments through FY-2030 totaling approximately $500,000."

### What the budget doesn't say:

**Every cellular meter requires a monthly cellular data subscription.** This is the hidden ongoing cost. Industry-standard cellular meter subscriptions run:

- **$2-5 per meter per month** for the cellular data plan (AT&T, Verizon, or T-Mobile)
- **Plus** monthly software/platform fees for the meter reading portal
- **Plus** ongoing maintenance and battery replacement (cellular meters have 10-15 year battery life)

### Estimated Annual Recurring Costs (once fully deployed):

| Cost Category | Low Estimate | High Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Cellular data plans (~1,598 meters x $2-5/mo) | $38,352/yr | $95,880/yr |
| Software platform/SaaS license | $5,000-15,000/yr | $15,000-25,000/yr |
| Maintenance/battery replacement | $5,000/yr | $15,000/yr |
| **Total estimated recurring** | **$48,000/yr** | **$136,000/yr** |

**These costs never go away.** They start small in FY-2026 (few meters deployed) and grow every year until the full system is operational. By FY-2030, the town will be paying $50-136K annually in perpetuity just for the subscription fees on meters that cost $500K to install.

### 10-Year Total Cost of Ownership

| Category | Cost |
|---|---|
| Hardware + installation (FY-2026-2030) | $500,000 |
| Cellular subscriptions (10 years, mid-estimate) | $670,000 |
| Software platform (10 years) | $150,000 |
| Maintenance/battery (10 years) | $100,000 |
| **Estimated 10-year TCO** | **$1,420,000** |

## What the Town Claims

From the budget narrative:
> "Cellular meters allow near real time consumption data, reduce manual labor, minimize billing errors, and help identify leaks that would otherwise result in lost water and lost revenue."

### The Other Side

1. **The existing radio-read meters work.** They were "already outdated when the last bulk purchase occurred in 2020/2021" — but the town has been reading meters just fine. The budget even talks about buying a new pickup truck specifically for meter reading routes.

2. **Why buy a meter-reading truck AND cellular meters?** If the meters will read themselves via cellular, why spend $80K on a meter-reading pickup? These are contradictory investments. Either you need staff driving around reading meters, or you don't.

3. **Cellular dependency.** The town is making its water billing system dependent on cellular carrier infrastructure. If AT&T or Verizon raises rates, changes coverage, or discontinues the IoT plan, the town has no fallback. Radio-read meters work independently of any carrier.

4. **Data privacy.** Cellular smart meters transmit real-time water usage data through third-party cellular networks and cloud platforms. This creates a detailed profile of each household's water consumption patterns — when people are home, when they're away, usage habits. There's no mention of a data privacy policy in the budget.

5. **Small town, big town solution.** Cellular AMI makes sense for utilities with 50,000+ meters where manual reading is genuinely impractical. For 1,598 meters in a town you can drive across in 5 minutes, the labor savings don't justify the subscription costs. The town's own budget describes using a dump truck for meter reading — a mid-size pickup and one staff member can read every meter in town in a day or two.

## The Pattern

This is the same pattern as Verkada (cloud cameras with mandatory subscriptions) and the street sweeper (lease-purchase creating multi-year payments). The town is systematically converting one-time capital costs into permanent recurring obligations:

| System | Capital Cost | Recurring Annual Cost | Creates Dependency On |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verkada cameras | ~$50K | $5-15K/yr cloud license | Verkada cloud platform |
| Cellular meters | $500K | $50-136K/yr subscriptions | Cellular carrier + SaaS platform |
| Street sweeper | $200-350K | $40-60K/yr lease payments | Lease financing company |

**Combined new recurring costs from these three items alone: $95-211K/year, permanently.**

## What Nobody's Asking

1. What is the actual cellular subscription cost per meter? (Not disclosed in the budget)
2. What carrier and what happens if they change pricing?
3. What is the SaaS platform and what are the contract terms?
4. Who owns the usage data? Where is it stored? Who has access?
5. What happens when the cellular network technology changes (3G→4G→5G)? Do all meters need replacing again?
6. Was a cost-benefit analysis done comparing cellular AMI to simply maintaining the radio-read system?
7. Did the Board see the full 10-year TCO before approving?

## Historical Context

- **2020/2021:** Town purchased radio-read meters (most recent bulk buy)
- **2019:** Catastrophic water loss event — the town's primary water problem is infrastructure, not metering
- **2025:** Water restrictions imposed — again, infrastructure-driven, not metering-driven
- **2026:** $50K initial cellular meter investment begins

The town's water crises have been caused by **pipe failures and tank failures**, not by inability to read meters. Spending $500K+ on meters while the distribution system is still being repaired is a questionable prioritization.
